Use a combinatorial proof to show that \[\sum_{k=0}^n \binom n k \binom{2n}{n-k} = \binom{3n}n.\]

Hidden.

 Suppose we have a binary string (a string over $\{0,1\}$) of length $3n$ that contains $n$ $0$s and $2n$ $1$s. We will show two ways to count these strings. We select $n$ positions to be filled with $0$s and the remaining positions are filled with $1$s. There are $\binom{3n}n$ such strings. We consider the first $n$ positions. From these $n$ positions, we choose $k$ to be filled with $0$s and the remaining positions are filled with $1$s. There are $\binom n k$ such choices. Now, consider the remaining $2n$ positions in the string. Since there are $n$ $0$s in total, we have $n-k$ remaining $0$s to place. There are $\binom{2n}{n-k}$ such choices. Therefore, there are $\binom n k \binom{2n}{n-k}$ strings with $k$ $0$'s in the first $n$ positions. But there can be anywhere from $0$ to $n$ $0$s in the first $n$ positions of the string. This gives us \[\sum_{k=0}^n \binom n k \binom{2n}{n-k}\] possible binary strings of length $3n$ that contain $n$ $0$s. Therefore, \[\sum_{k=0}^n \binom n k \binom{2n}{n-k} = \binom{3n}n.\]
