Let $T $ be a binary tree with height $h \in \mathbb N$. Then $T$ has at most $2^h - 1$ nodes.

Hidden.

 We will prove this by strong induction on $h$, the height of $T$. Base case. We have $h = 0$. Then $T$ is empty. Therefore, $T$ has no nodes and $0 = 1-1 \leq 2^0 - 1$. Inductive case. Let $k \in \mathbb N$. Suppose all binary trees of height $j \leq k$ have at most $2^j - 1$ nodes. Consider a binary tree $T$ with height $k+1$. Since $T$ is nonempty, $T$ contains a node and two subtrees, call them $T_1$ and $T_2$. The subtrees $T_1$ and $T_2$ have height at most $k$. By our inductive hypothesis, $T_1$ and $T_2$ both contain at most $2^k - 1$ nodes each. The total number of nodes in $T$ is exactly one more than the number of nodes in $T_1$ and $T_2$. Then the number of nodes in $T$ is at most $1 + (2^k-1) + (2^k-1)$. Then $1 + (2^k-1) + (2^k-1) = 2 \cdot (2^k-1) + 1 = 2^{k+1} - 1$. Therefore, $T$ has at most $2^{k+1} - 1$ nodes.
