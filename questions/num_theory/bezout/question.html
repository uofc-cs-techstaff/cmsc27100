Recall Bézout's lemma: For all integers $m$ and $n$, there exist integers $a$ and $b$ such that $\gcd(m,n) = a \cdot m + b \cdot n$.

A common pitfall in applying this theorem is that students often take the converse to be true—that if there exist integers $a$ and $b$ such that $d = a \cdot m + b \cdot n$, then $d = \gcd(m,n)$.

Show that this is not true. Let $m = {{params.m}}$ and $n = {{params.n}}$. Give an example of integers $a,b,c$ such that $c = a \cdot m + b \cdot n$, but $c$ is not the greatest common divisor of $a$ and $b$.
