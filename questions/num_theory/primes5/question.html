Let $N$ be an integer that can be written as $4m+3$, where $m \in \mathbb N$. Prove that $N$ has a prime factor of the same form (i.e. one of the prime factors can be written as $4\ell+3$, for some $k \in \mathbb N$).

Hidden.

 Since $N = 4m+3$, we have $N = 3 \pmod 4$. Let $N = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k$ be the prime factorization of $N$, with $p_i$ prime for $1 \leq i \leq k$. We know that $p_i \neq 0, 2 \pmod 4$, since $N$ is odd. Therefore, $p_i = 1 \pmod 4$ or $p_i = 3 \pmod 4$. Suppose that none of the prime factors $p_i$'s are of the form $4\ell+3$. Then $p_i = 1 \pmod 4$ for all $1 \leq i \leq k$. Then $p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k = 1 \pmod 4$. But this contradicts our assumption that $N = p_1 \dots p_k = 3 \pmod 4$. Therefore, our assumption was incorrect and we must have a prime factor of the form $4\ell + 3$.
