We say that an integer $a$ is a perfect square if there exists an integer $b$ such that $a = b^2$. Prove that if $m$ and $n$ are perfect squares, then $m \cdot n$ is also a perfect square.

Suppose $m$ is a perfect square. There exists an integer $s$ such that $m = s^2$. Suppose $n$ is a perfect square. There exists an integer $t$ such that $n = t^2$. Then $m \cdot n = s^2 \cdot t^2 = (s \cdot s) (t \cdot t) = (s \cdot t) (s \cdot t) = (s \cdot t)^2$. Since $s$ and $t$ are integers, $s \cdot t$ is an integer. Since $s \cdot t$ is an integer, $m \cdot n$ is a perfect square.
