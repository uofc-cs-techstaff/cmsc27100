Consider a sequence of integers defined by

  • $y_0 = 1$,
  • $y_1 = 0$,
  • $y_n = y_{n-2}$ for $n \geq 2$.

Prove that $y_n = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^n)$ for all $n \in \mathbb N$.

Hidden.

 We will prove this by strong induction on $n$. Base case. If $n = 0$, then $\frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^n) = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + 1) = 1 = y_0$. If $n = 1$, then $\frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^n) = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 - 1) = 0 = y_1$. Therefore, the base cases $n = 0,1$ hold. Inductive case. Let $k \in \mathbb N$ with $k \geq 2$. Assume that for all $0 \leq j \leq k$, $y_k = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^k)$. Consider $n = k+1$. By definition, $y_{k+1} = y_{k-1}$. By our inductive hypothesis, $y_{k-1} = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^{k-1})$. Then $\frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^{k-1}) = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^{k-1} \cdot (-1)^2) = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^{k+1})$. Therefore, the inductive case holds. Therefore, $y_n = \frac 1 2 \cdot (1 + (-1)^n)$ for all natural numbers $n$.
