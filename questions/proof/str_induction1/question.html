Prove that every natural number $n \geq 44$ can be written as $n = 20a + 9b + 6c$ for some $a,b,c \in \mathbb N$.

Note. This is an instance of the Frobenius problem, due to Ferdinand Frobenius in the late 1800s. In general, the Frobenius problem asks, given positive relatively prime integers $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$, what is the largest integer not representable as a non-negative integer linear combination of the $x_i$'s. This specific instance is due to Picciotto in the 1980s, when McNuggets in the UK came in boxes of 6, 9, and 20.

Hidden.

 We will prove this by strong induction on $n$. Base case. We have \begin{align*} 44 &= 20 \cdot 1 + 9 \cdot 0 + 6 \cdot 4 \\ 45 &= 20 \cdot 0 + 9 \cdot 3 + 6 \cdot 3 \\ 46 &= 20 \cdot 2 + 9 \cdot 0 + 6 \cdot 1 \\ 47 &= 20 \cdot 1 + 9 \cdot 3 + 6 \cdot 0 \\ 48 &= 20 \cdot 0 + 9 \cdot 0 + 6 \cdot 8 \\ 49 &= 20 \cdot 2 + 9 \cdot 1 + 6 \cdot 0 \\ \end{align*} Therefore, the base case holds. Inductive case. Let $k \in \mathbb N$ with $k \geq 50$. Assume that for all $44 \leq j \leq k$ there exist $a,b,c \in \mathbb N$ such that $j = 20a + 9b + 6c$. Consider $n = k+1$. Since $k \geq 50$, we know $n = k+1 \geq 51$. Then $44 \leq k-6 \lt k$. We can apply our inductive hypothesis to $k-6$. By our inductive hypothesis, there exist natural numbers $a,b,c$ such that $k-6 = 20a + 9b + 6c$. Then $k+1 = 20a + 9b + 6(c+1)$. Therefore, the inductive case holds. Therefore, if $n \geq 44$, there are natural numbers $a,b,c$ such that $n = 20a + 9b + 6c$. Let $n = 0$.
