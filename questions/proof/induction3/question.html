Let $p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n$ be propositions for $n \geq 1$. Prove that \[\neg \left( \bigwedge_{i=1}^n p_i \right) = \bigvee_{i=1}^n \neg p_i.\]

Note. The big $\wedge$ and $\vee$ are analogs of the summation symbol $\sum$ in that we're taking the conjunction or disjunction of the indexed terms.

 We will prove this by induction on $n$. Base case. Let $n = 1$. Then $\neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^1 p_i \right) = \neg p_1 = \bigvee\limits_{i=1}^1 \neg p_i$. Therefore, the base case holds. Inductive case. Let $k \in \mathbb N$. Assume that $\neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^k p_i \right) = \bigvee\limits_{i=1}^k \neg p_i$. Consider $n = k+1$. Then $\neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^{k+1} p_i \right) = \neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^{k} p_i \wedge p_{k+1} \right)$. By De Morgan's laws, $\neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^{k} p_i \wedge p_{k+1} \right) = \neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^{k} p_i \right) \vee \neg p_{k+1}$. By our inductive hypothesis, $\neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^{k} p_i \right) \vee \neg p_{k+1} = \bigvee\limits_{i=1}^{k} \neg p_i \vee \neg p_{k+1}$. Then $\bigvee\limits_{i=1}^{k} \neg p_i \vee \neg p_{k+1} = \bigvee\limits_{i=1}^{k+1} \neg p_i$. Therefore, the inductive case holds. Therefore, $\neg \left( \bigwedge\limits_{i=1}^n p_i \right) = \bigvee\limits_{i=1}^n \neg p_i$ holds for all $n \in \mathbb N$. Let $n = 0$.
